What this is

The desk's note-taking assistant for reading the coffee market: it tells you which questions matter, keeps your notes clean and readable, and turns each one into a record you can learn from.

Most market notes are scattered, half-finished, and impossible to read back a week later. This fixes that, and does more. It walks you through what actually matters this week and lets you skip what does not, so nothing important is missed. It turns your thinking into one clean, structured note that is easy to scan and share. And it asks you to commit, bullish or bearish, with the reasoning written beside it, which is the part that makes note-keeping actually teach: you take a real position, you keep a clear trail, and when the market proves you out you can see exactly what to fix. The fastest, clearest way to take notes on this market, and the only one that helps you get better at reading it.

What this means and why

The reasoning behind each question

Plain-language background for every topic in the walk-through. Read it once to learn the ground, or jump straight to a section using the link inside each stage above. This is the part to hand the next person first.

▲
Stage 1 · the market's story

Why start with the story?

Coffee is never trading on everything at once. In any given week one or two things dominate, and everything else is background. It might be a frost scare in Brazil, rain in Colombia, a draw in exchange stocks, or the funds piling in. The single most useful habit is to name that dominant story before you look at anything else, because it tells you what actually matters today and what is just noise.

A new person tends to weigh everything equally and gets lost. An experienced trader knows that most days, one theme is driving price and the rest barely moves it. If you cannot name the story, that is a real finding too, it usually means the market is drifting without a clear driver, which is itself worth writing down.

The point: name what the market is fixated on first, then spend the rest of the walk checking whether you agree with that story or are leaning against it.

Stage 2 · weather, by origin

Why weather, and why only sometimes?

Weather is the biggest source of genuine supply surprise in coffee, a frost or a drought can take real volume out of the market fast. But it only matters at certain points in each country's calendar, and most of the year most origins are not in a window where weather can do damage. The skill is not knowing that weather matters in general, it is knowing whether it matters right now, and where.

Brazil is the giant: arabica and conilon, with the frost window in June to August and flowering in September to October, the two moments the whole market watches. Colombia grows washed arabica with two crops a year, where too much rain hurts the main mid-year crop and disrupts flowering. Vietnam is the robusta heavyweight, where the dry-season irrigation window early in the year and the late-year harvest are what count. Central America and Ethiopia add washed and natural arabica, sensitive around their harvests and seasonal rains.

Robusta and arabica are different markets that influence each other. A robusta problem in Vietnam can pull arabica up through substitution, roasters shifting blends, so origins do not sit in neat boxes.

The point: decide if any origin is in a sensitive window this month first. If none is, weather is probably not the story, and saying so is a real decision, not a gap.

Stage 3 · physical supply

What the physical picture is telling you

This is the desk's home ground: the real question of whether coffee is actually moving and reaching the market, as opposed to what the futures screen is doing. Several signals read this, and the trap is that they often move together, so it is easy to mistake one story told four ways for four independent confirmations.

Certified stocks are the coffee sitting in exchange-approved warehouses, ready to deliver against futures. Falling stocks usually mean tightness, less buffer available. The curve structure, the price difference between nearby and later months, is a powerful tell. When the nearby month trades above the later one it is called backwardation, and it signals tightness now: people are paying a premium to get coffee sooner. When a spread closes firmer, the number has moved up, so a backwardation getting firmer means the nearby is pulling even further ahead, tightening more. Commercialisation is how much of their crop farmers have sold; when they hold back, supply tightens. And the Brazilian real drives Brazilian selling, a weaker real makes export dollars worth more locally, so farmers sell, while a stronger real makes them hold.

The point: certified stocks, the curve, and the real often all say tight at the same time because they are reading the same tightness. Ask whether your signals genuinely agree or are echoing, before you count them as strong confirmation.

Stage 4 · price action

When the chart matters, and when it doesn't

Price action is what the chart is doing, levels, momentum, where price is stalling or breaking. It can be useful for timing, but in a market being driven by a real fundamental story, the chart is often just noise that follows the news rather than leading it. Plenty of experienced traders barely look at momentum indicators, and a unanimous-looking technical picture can mislead because the common ones are all calculated off the same price series, so they tend to agree by construction, not because they are independent.

That is why the honest first question is not what the chart is saying, but whether the chart deserves a vote in this particular market at all. In a fundamentally driven market, the answer is often no, and setting it aside on purpose is a legitimate, common call, not laziness.

The point: ask whether the price action earns a place today before reading anything into it. Use it as a timing aid layered on the fundamentals, never as a standalone reason on its own.

Stage 5 · positioning & macro

Who holds the market, and the backdrop

Positioning means how the speculative funds are placed, which you read from the COT (Commitment of Traders) report. The key insight is to read it as fragility, not direction. When funds are heavily net long after a rally, that is a lot of selling waiting to happen if anything disappoints, the risk is asymmetric to the downside. When they are heavily net short, the market is squeeze fuel, any bullish surprise forces them to buy back fast. A crowded position is a contrarian risk, not a continuation signal. The COT is also reported with a few days' lag, so it tells you where the crowd was, not exactly where it is.

Macro is the broad backdrop, mainly the US dollar and the general risk tone. Coffee is priced in dollars, so a firm dollar is a mild headwind and a weak one a mild tailwind, and a risk-off mood can pull money out of commodities generally. But these rarely lead coffee, they shape how a move plays out rather than causing it.

The point: positioning and macro are around the edges, worth a glance, rarely the main event. Read positioning as how fragile the market is, not which way it points.

Stage 6 · the view

Committing, and naming how you'd be wrong

After walking the topics, you state your overall read in one line. That part is straightforward. The part that matters most, and the part new people skip, is the next question: what would have to happen for this view to be wrong?

This is called the disconfirmation question, and it is the single most valuable habit in the whole tool. Writing down the specific thing that would break your view does two things. It forces you to hold your own idea honestly rather than just looking for reasons you are right, and it gives you something concrete to watch. Then, when the market surprises you, you can look back at exactly the condition you named and see whether that is what failed. That is the difference between being wrong and confused, and being wrong and learning. Naming your single biggest uncertainty does the same job, it marks where the soft ground was, for you and for the next person reading your note.

The point: a view you cannot disprove is not a view, it is a hope. Always write down what would make you wrong, and what you are least sure about. That is what turns a wrong call into a lesson.
